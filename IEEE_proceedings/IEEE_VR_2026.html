 Shadowless Projection Mapping for Tabletop Workspaces with Synthetic Aperture Projector
· Author List
Takahiro Okamoto: The University of Osaka; Masaki Takeuchi: The University of Osaka; Masataka Sawayama: Hokkaido University; Daisuke Iwai: The University of Osaka
· Abstract ▼
Conventional projection mapping (PM) often produce distracting cast shadows when users occlude the light path. We present a synthetic-aperture PM system that densely arranges many projectors to achieve delay-free, shadowless projection without computational occlusion compensation. To mitigate spatial resolution loss from subpixel misalignment across overlaid projections, we develop and validate an offline blur compensation method whose computation time is independent of the number of projectors. We also show that shadowless PM is critical for material appearance manipulation without evoking projection-like impression. We define this as "sense of projection (SoP)" and propose a PM design framework based on user studies to minimize the SoP.

 High-Contrast Projection Mapping under Light Field Illumination with LED Display and Aperiodic Lens Array
· Author List
Kotaro Fujimura: The University of Osaka; Hiroki Kusuyama: The University of Osaka; Masaki Takeuchi: The University of Osaka; Daisuke Iwai: The University of Osaka
· Abstract ▼
We propose target-excluding lighting to overcome dark-room constraints in Projection Mapping (PM). Combining an LED display with an optimized aperiodic lens array, our system achieves light field illumination selectively lighting the environment while avoiding the PM target. This compact architecture provides a large effective light source area, reproducing natural soft shadows comparable to typical lighting, maintaining spatial controllability for precise avoidance. We introduce a computational technique optimizing aperiodic lens placement to suppress crosstalk-induced dark spots, and efficient methods for computing LED luminance patterns. Experiments with our prototype demonstrate high-contrast, dynamic PM in bright environments.
· Paper ID: P1060
 DiffPC: Diffusion-Based Projector Photometric Compensation
· Author List
Yuxi Wang: Hangzhou Dianzi University; Haibin Ling: Westlake University; Bingyao Huang: Southwest University
· Abstract ▼
Projector photometric compensation aims to mitigate color distortions introduced by surface textures, irregular material reflectance, and environmental lighting. Since existing deep-learning methods rely on laborious scene-specific data and often overlook perceptual quality, this paper presents a diffusion-based framework that reformulates compensation as a constrained denoising task. By modeling distortions as environment-dependent additive noise, this paper iteratively recovers compensation images using photometry-aware and content conditions. Experiments demonstrate that the proposed approach has strong generalization capabilities and achieves superior perceptual visual quality over prior art.

· Author List

· Abstract ▼
Projector compensation aims to correct distortions on nonplanar or textured surfaces, but most methods require retraining whenever the environment changes.  Eliciting Pleasantness with Haptic Feedback: The Role of Physical and Pseudo-Haptic Resistance in Virtual Archery
· Author List
Yusuke Ujitoko: NTT, Inc.; Yuki Ban: The University of Tokyo
· Abstract ▼
Although haptic technologies that allow users to touch virtual objects have been actively researched in the academic field, they are not yet widely adopted in real-world applications. To promote broader adoption, designing haptic stimuli that elicit pleasant sensations is essential since hedonic motivation significantly drives user acceptance. However, how such pleasantness can be effectively induced through haptic stimulation during a user's active exploration remains an open question. In this study, we investigated the relationship between the applied force during user actions and the resulting pleasantness, using a bow-dragging and arrow-shooting scenario. Specifically, we conducted three psychophysical experiments to investigate two types of force feedback. One type was a physical force that stimulated the kinesthetic sense, and the other was a pseudo force induced through visual manipulation. Our results revealed that pleasantness tended to be elicited when physical resistance force was present during bow dragging, whereas this tendency was not observed for pseudo resistance force. Our findings show that the two types of force are not interchangeable for eliciting positive affect. We also found that the arrow's flying speed contributed to pleasantness as much as the physical resistance force, suggesting that pleasantness is influenced postdictively. Moreover, across all experiments, perceived pleasantness was strongly correlated with the perceived naturalness of the bow-and-arrow behavior. These findings provide valuable insights for designing pleasant interactions involving continuous force feedback, highlighting the distinct roles of physical and pseudo-haptic cues.
